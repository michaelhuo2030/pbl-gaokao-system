# Test-to-Problem Transformation Methodology

## The Core Mechanism

Every 高考 knowledge point can be transformed into a problem worth solving. This document describes how to perform that transformation systematically.

## The Transformation Formula

```
Knowledge Point + Real Context + Student Agency = PBL Problem
```

### Step 1: Identify the Knowledge Point

Start with the official curriculum standard. Be specific:
- NOT "students learn about quadratic functions"
- YES "students can solve quadratic equations, graph parabolas, identify vertex/axis of symmetry, apply to optimization problems" (高中数学必修1, 函数章节)

### Step 2: Find the Real Context

Ask: "Where does this knowledge actually matter in the world?"

Every piece of knowledge in the 高考 syllabus exists because someone, somewhere, needed it to solve a real problem. Find that origin:

| Knowledge Point | Real-World Origin |
|---|---|
| Quadratic functions | Optimization (maximize area, minimize cost, projectile motion) |
| 文言文 reading | Understanding historical documents, classical literature, legal traditions |
| Chemical equations | Predicting reactions in manufacturing, cooking, environmental science |
| English reading comprehension | Accessing international information, research, communication |
| Probability | Decision-making under uncertainty, risk assessment, game design |
| Geography of China | Understanding regional economic differences, urban planning, agriculture |

### Step 3: Frame as a Problem the Student Owns

Transform the context into a problem statement that:
- Has no single "right answer" approach (even if the underlying knowledge is precise)
- Requires the student to investigate before they can solve
- Produces an artifact (report, design, prototype, presentation)
- Allows the student to say "I figured this out"

**Template**: "You are [role]. You face [real situation]. Use [investigation/analysis/design] to [produce artifact]."

### Step 4: Verify Coverage

Check: does engaging with this problem require mastering the target knowledge point?

- If YES → the transformation works
- If PARTIALLY → add a complementary problem or scaffolding activity
- If NO → the problem is interesting but doesn't serve the curriculum goal; find a different context

### Step 5: Design the Scaffolding (20%)

Once the problem is framed (80% of design effort), add just enough scaffolding:
- What prerequisite skills do students need?
- What resources should be available?
- What checkpoints ensure students don't get stuck?
- What direct instruction moments are needed? (Yes, some direct teaching is appropriate — within a problem context)

## Quality Criteria for Transformed Problems

A good PBL problem:

1. **Cannot be solved by Googling the answer** — requires synthesis and judgment
2. **Requires the target knowledge** — not just tangentially related
3. **Has natural differentiation** — students can engage at different depths
4. **Produces evidence of understanding** — the artifact demonstrates mastery
5. **Is genuinely interesting** — to at least 70% of students in the target age range
6. **Connects to student life or aspirations** — relevance is felt, not explained
7. **Allows "我可以" moments** — students discover capability through the work

## Common Transformation Patterns

### Pattern A: "You Are the Expert"

Frame students as professionals who need this knowledge:

- "You are an engineer designing..." (Physics, Math)
- "You are a journalist investigating..." (语文, History, Politics)
- "You are a data scientist analyzing..." (Math, Geography)
- "You are a doctor explaining..." (Biology, Chemistry)

### Pattern B: "Something is Wrong"

Present a mystery or problem to diagnose:

- "This bridge design failed. Why?" (Physics)
- "This historical account contradicts this one. Which is accurate?" (History, 文言文)
- "This experiment gave unexpected results. What happened?" (Chemistry, Biology)
- "This business is losing money despite high sales. Why?" (Math, Economics)

### Pattern C: "Design a Better One"

Challenge students to improve something real:

- "Design a more efficient [water heater / battery / garden layout]" (Science, Math)
- "Rewrite this explanation so a younger student can understand it" (语文, any subject)
- "Design a fair voting system for [school decision]" (Math, Politics)

### Pattern D: "What If?"

Counterfactual or scenario-based exploration:

- "What if China's geography were different? How would agriculture change?" (Geography)
- "What if this chemical reaction ran at twice the temperature?" (Chemistry)
- "What if this poem were written today? How would it be different?" (语文)

### Pattern E: "Teach It"

The ultimate test of understanding:

- "Create a 5-minute lesson on [topic] for students two years younger"
- "Write a children's book that explains [scientific concept]"
- "Design a game that teaches [math concept]"

## Handling "Hard to Transform" Topics

Some knowledge points seem resistant to PBL transformation:

### Memorization-Heavy Content (Characters, Vocabulary, Formulas, Dates)

**Solution**: Embed in production contexts. Students learn characters they NEED for their writing projects. Formulas are derived or discovered through problem-solving, then practiced for fluency.

**Supplementary**: Spaced repetition practice (can be AI-assisted) runs alongside projects. This is the 20% scaffolding — efficient, targeted, not the main event.

### Highly Abstract Topics (Limits, Complex Numbers, Abstract Grammar)

**Solution**: Historical or application framing. "Why did mathematicians invent complex numbers? What problem were they trying to solve?" Students retrace the intellectual journey.

### Exam-Specific Formats (Cloze Tests, Multiple Choice Strategies)

**Solution**: These ARE a skill — and they're practiced directly in exam synthesis sessions. The knowledge comes from PBL; the format is trained separately. Like an athlete who trains in the gym (PBL) and practices on the race course (exam drills).
