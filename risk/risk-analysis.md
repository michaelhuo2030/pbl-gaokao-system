# Risk Analysis

## Top Risks and Mitigations

### Risk 1: 高考 Score Disadvantage

**Severity**: Critical (system-killing if true)
**Probability**: Medium

**Description**: PBL students score lower on 高考 than traditionally-educated peers, undermining the system's core claim.

**Root Causes**:
- Knowledge coverage gaps from insufficiently mapped PBL problems
- Exam format unfamiliarity (speed, question types, tricks)
- Depth-over-breadth tradeoff that leaves some topics undertreated

**Mitigations**:
1. **Rigorous coverage mapping** — Every knowledge point explicitly mapped to PBL problems (see mapping docs). No hand-waving.
2. **Monthly diagnostics** — Continuous gap detection from Grade 1 onward
3. **Gap sprint protocol** — Dedicated remediation period in Grade 11
4. **Dedicated exam training** — 18 months of format-specific preparation (Grade 11 Sem 2 through Grade 12)
5. **Pilot data** — Run controlled comparisons before scaling. If data doesn't support the thesis, revise.

**Monitoring**: Track practice exam scores against regional averages monthly in Grade 11-12.

---

### Risk 2: Parent Resistance

**Severity**: High (adoption-blocking)
**Probability**: High

**Description**: Parents don't trust PBL to prepare their children for 高考. They demand traditional drilling or withdraw children.

**Root Causes**:
- 高考 stakes are existentially high for Chinese families
- "Everyone else is drilling" creates arms-race psychology
- Unfamiliar approach triggers anxiety
- Results lag: PBL benefits may not be visible on standard metrics until later

**Mitigations**:
1. **Transparent curriculum mapping** — Show parents exactly how each 高考 topic is covered
2. **Regular diagnostic reports** — Monthly data showing knowledge coverage
3. **Parent education program** — Workshops on learning science, PBL evidence, motivation
4. **Early wins** — Show concrete project outputs that demonstrate capability
5. **Comparison data** — As cohorts progress, publish honest comparison with traditional outcomes
6. **Parent journaling option** — Parents who journal observations of their child's motivation and capability become allies (they see the transformation firsthand)

**The honest conversation**: "We understand your anxiety. Here's how we ensure your child is exam-ready. Let's track together."

---

### Risk 3: Teacher Capability Gap

**Severity**: High (quality-determining)
**Probability**: High

**Description**: Teachers trained in 应试 methods cannot design and facilitate PBL effectively.

**Root Causes**:
- Chinese teacher training emphasizes content delivery, not facilitation
- PBL requires improvisation, domain depth, and comfort with ambiguity
- Teachers are evaluated on student test scores → incentive misalignment

**Mitigations**:
1. **Teacher training program** (separate project, but critical)
2. **Problem design support** — Provide detailed problem sets so teachers don't start from scratch
3. **AI-assisted facilitation** — AI helps teachers design differentiated problems and track coverage
4. **Mentor teachers** — Experienced PBL facilitators coach newer ones
5. **Gradual transition** — Schools don't flip overnight; phase PBL in across grades

**Note**: This risk is the primary reason for detailed mapping documents — they reduce the design burden on individual teachers.

---

### Risk 4: Motivation Doesn't Transfer to Exam Performance

**Severity**: Medium-High
**Probability**: Medium

**Description**: Students are motivated and capable but don't translate this into exam scores. They resist the "gear shift" to exam preparation.

**Root Causes**:
- Students who love PBL may resist standardized testing as "beneath them"
- The transition from open-ended projects to closed-format exams is jarring
- Some students may not take exam prep seriously enough

**Mitigations**:
1. **Transparent framing** — From the beginning, students understand that 高考 is part of the system. Not the enemy, not the goal — a milestone.
2. **Exam as strategy game** — Frame exam prep as an intellectual challenge, not drudgery. "How do test designers think? How do you outsmart the test?"
3. **Gradual transition** — Exam-format elements appear from Grade 10, not suddenly in Grade 12
4. **Agency in exam prep** — Students own their preparation strategy. This IS 主体性 in action.
5. **Role models** — PBL graduates who excelled on 高考 AND in university/career

---

### Risk 5: The "Island School" Problem

**Severity**: Medium
**Probability**: Medium

**Description**: This system works in isolated schools but can't integrate with the broader 体制内 ecosystem (transfers, recognition, peer schools).

**Root Causes**:
- Non-standard assessment doesn't translate to standard grade reports
- Transfer students may struggle to adapt (in either direction)
- Other schools and parents may view PBL graduates suspiciously

**Mitigations**:
1. **Dual reporting** — Maintain standard grade reports alongside portfolio assessment
2. **Curriculum alignment documentation** — Always demonstrate compliance with national standards
3. **Transfer protocols** — Explicit support for students entering or leaving the system
4. **Results speak** — 高考 scores provide the ultimate integration metric

---

### Risk 6: AI Dependency

**Severity**: Medium
**Probability**: Medium-High (increasing over time)

**Description**: Students use AI as a crutch rather than a tool, undermining both 主体性 and genuine understanding.

**Root Causes**:
- AI makes it easy to produce work without understanding
- Detection of AI-generated work becomes increasingly difficult
- Students under pressure will take shortcuts

**Mitigations**:
1. **Process over product** — Assessment includes process documentation (journals, drafts), not just final products
2. **Oral defense** — Students must explain and defend their work verbally. AI can't do this for you.
3. **"Without AI" checkpoints** — Regular skill demonstrations without AI access
4. **Transparency norms** — AI use is documented, not hidden. Hiding AI use is a norm violation, not a crime.
5. **The identity test** — Journaling regularly asks: "Could I do this without AI?" Students self-monitor.

---

### Risk 7: Equity and Access

**Severity**: High
**Probability**: High

**Description**: PBL requires resources (time, materials, technology, trained teachers) that disadvantaged schools lack. The system becomes another privilege marker.

**Root Causes**:
- PBL is more resource-intensive than textbook + lecture
- Rural and underfunded schools can't implement the full system
- Digital divide affects AI integration

**Mitigations**:
1. **Low-resource variants** — Design PBL problems that work with minimal materials
2. **Community-as-resource** — Local problems ARE the curriculum; rural contexts provide different but equally rich problems
3. **Shared problem banks** — Open-source problem sets reduce design burden
4. **Virtual simulations and AI** — Technology can provide experiences that geography limits (virtual lab access, AI tutoring, virtual professional exposure)
5. **Transportation innovation** — "Cheat transportation" concept: subsidized travel so children can experience different environments regardless of where they live
6. **Society-as-village philosophy** — The broader community treats all children as their own, providing mentorship, exposure, and support across economic boundaries

---

### Risk 8: The System Depends on Extraordinary People

**Severity**: Medium
**Probability**: Medium

**Description**: The system works when designed and run by passionate, skilled people but fails in ordinary conditions.

**Root Causes**:
- PBL facilitation is harder than lecturing
- Motivation architecture requires constant attention
- Journaling systems require genuine engagement from mentors

**Mitigations**:
1. **Systematize what can be systematized** — Problem banks, mapping documents, assessment rubrics reduce the creativity burden
2. **AI assistance for teachers** — AI helps design problems, track coverage, analyze journals, suggest interventions
3. **Clear protocols** — The system documents specifically what to do at each stage
4. **Community of practice** — Teachers learn from each other; no school implements alone

---

## The Liberation Worldview: Why These Risks Are Worth Taking

The traditional system's risk is certainty: it reliably produces stressed, compliance-oriented students with knowledge they'll forget and motivation they've lost. It is a known quantity of human potential wasted.

This system's risks are uncertain: it MIGHT produce lower exam scores, MIGHT face parent resistance, MIGHT require resources that don't exist everywhere. These risks are real and must be mitigated seriously.

But the upside is transformative: a generation of students who are capable, motivated, collaborative, and see the world as a non-zero-sum game. Students who believe "我可以" and have evidence. Students who use AI as amplifiers. Students who see each other as allies, not competitors.

**跨越式发展一代人** — leapfrog development of an entire generation — is possible if:
- The knowledge coverage is rigorous (no exam disadvantage)
- The motivation architecture works (intrinsic drive sustained)
- AI and technology provide access (virtual simulations, transportation, shared resources)
- Society adopts the village mindset (every child is our child)

The risks are manageable. The cost of NOT trying is a continuation of the current trajectory: high test scores, broken humans.
