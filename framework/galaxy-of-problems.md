# The Galaxy of Problems

## The Vision

Imagine a galaxy — not of stars, but of problems.

Each problem is a point of light. Some are bright and close (immediate, tangible, personally relevant). Some are distant and faint (abstract, frontier, waiting to be discovered). New problems appear as the student grows — the galaxy expands.

A child enters this galaxy at age 2 and begins exploring. At first, the nearby problems are simple, sensory, concrete: "Why does this float?" "What happens if I mix these colors?" "How many steps to the door?"

As they grow, they travel further. The problems become richer, more interconnected. "Why does the bridge hold weight?" connects to "What makes materials strong?" connects to "How do you design things that don't fail?" Each solved problem reveals new ones, like a game that never ends because the map keeps growing.

By age 18, the student has traversed a unique path through the galaxy. No two paths are identical — because no two humans are identical in their curiosity, their context, their gifts. But every path has covered the necessary territory (高考 alignment), because the galaxy was designed so that ALL essential knowledge points appear as problems along the way.

**This is education as exploration, not education as marathon. The game of life, played with problems.**

## How the Galaxy Works

### 1. Problems Are the Fundamental Unit

Not lessons. Not chapters. Not subjects. **Problems.**

A problem is:
- A situation that requires investigation
- An outcome that requires creation
- A question that demands an answer the student doesn't yet have
- A challenge that builds capability through engagement

Problems are categorized not by subject (math, science, 语文) but by:
- **Scale**: tiny (one session) → small (one week) → medium (one month) → large (one semester) → epic (year-long)
- **Domain cluster**: where in the knowledge universe it lives
- **Required knowledge**: what 高考 curriculum points it activates
- **Connection density**: how many other problems it connects to
- **Accessibility**: what prior knowledge/skill is needed to engage

### 2. The Galaxy Has Structure (But Feels Like Freedom)

Behind the scenes, the galaxy is mapped to curriculum requirements. But from the student's perspective, it's just an expanding universe of interesting things to figure out.

```
Student sees:  "Cool problem! Let me investigate this."
System knows:  "This problem covers quadratic functions, 说明文 writing,
                and experimental design — 3 high-priority knowledge points
                for this semester."
```

The mapping is invisible to the student. The engagement is real. The coverage is guaranteed.

### 3. Problems Connect to Each Other

Solving one problem reveals new ones:

```
"Why does bread rise?"
    → "What is yeast?" (biology)
    → "What is fermentation?" (chemistry)
    → "How does temperature affect the process?" (physics + math modeling)
    → "How did ancient people discover fermentation?" (history)
    → "Can I write a guide for making perfect bread?" (语文: 说明文)
    → "What other fermented foods exist across cultures?" (geography + culture)
    → "Can I design a better bread recipe using data?" (math: optimization)
```

Each node in the galaxy leads to others. The student chooses which thread to follow. ALL threads cover curriculum. The student's path is unique; the destination (knowledge coverage) is shared.

### 4. The Galaxy Grows With the Student

#### Ages 2-5: The Neighborhood Galaxy
Problems are local, sensory, immediate:
- "Why is the shadow moving?"
- "How many seeds are in this watermelon?"
- "What does this feel like?"
- "Can I build a tower that doesn't fall?"

The galaxy is small, warm, close. Every problem can be explored by touching, seeing, tasting, building.

#### Ages 6-9: The City Galaxy
Problems expand to include structured investigation:
- "How does our neighborhood water system work?"
- "What lives in the soil in our garden?"
- "How far is it to school, measured in different ways?"
- "Why do some stories make you feel things?"

The galaxy has neighborhoods and districts. Students begin to see connections between areas.

#### Ages 10-12: The Country Galaxy
Problems become multi-dimensional, cross-subject:
- "Why does our city flood? Can we design a solution?"
- "What happened in this historical period, and why does it matter now?"
- "How do you feed a city? Where does the food come from?"
- "What makes a bridge strong enough to trust?"

The galaxy has regions, each rich with interconnected problems. Students plan expeditions.

#### Ages 13-15: The World Galaxy
Problems become genuinely complex, research-worthy:
- "How is AI changing employment in our region?"
- "What are the genetics of this hereditary condition?"
- "What energy system should our province adopt for 2050?"
- "How does propaganda work, historically and today?"

The galaxy has continents and oceans. Students navigate with growing independence.

#### Ages 16-18: The Frontier Galaxy
Problems become frontier-level, capstone-worthy:
- "What ethical framework should govern gene editing?"
- "How should China balance economic growth with environmental sustainability?"
- "What will education look like in 2050?"
- "How do we design cities for an aging population?"

The galaxy extends to the edges of human knowledge. Some problems have no known solutions. Students contribute to the frontier, not just traverse known territory.

## The Game Mechanics

### Why It Feels Like a Game

The galaxy of problems has natural game dynamics:

1. **Exploration**: Always something new to discover
2. **Progression**: Problems naturally scaffold — solving earlier ones enables later ones
3. **Choice**: Multiple paths through the galaxy; no single "correct" route
4. **Achievement**: Each solved problem produces a visible artifact (the portfolio)
5. **Connection**: Problems link to each other; solving one opens new ones
6. **Mastery**: Returning to a domain at a higher level reveals deeper problems
7. **Social**: Some problems are solo expeditions; others are group quests
8. **Stakes**: The problems are REAL — they matter beyond the classroom

### What Makes It Different From Gamification

This is NOT gamification (no points, badges, leaderboards, fake rewards). The "game" is the genuine experience of:
- Encountering something you don't understand
- Deciding to figure it out
- Struggling productively
- Breaking through to understanding
- Seeing new problems revealed by your understanding
- Repeating — forever

This is the game of intellectual life. It's the game you play naturally at age 3. It's the game the traditional system destroys by age 13. It's the game you rediscover at age 40 if you're lucky.

**This system never lets it stop.**

### The Personal Path

Every student's journey through the galaxy is unique:

**Student A** (loves building things):
```
Shadow exploration → Block towers → Bridge challenge → City design →
Sustainable building → Energy systems → Engineering capstone
```

**Student B** (loves stories and people):
```
Story time → Class newspaper → Historical mystery → Interview project →
Documentary investigation → Social policy research → Journalism capstone
```

**Student C** (loves nature and patterns):
```
Bug collection → Garden ecology → Weather tracking → Ecosystem audit →
Genetic investigation → Environmental modeling → Climate research capstone
```

ALL three paths cover the same 高考 knowledge points — through different problems, in different orders, with different emphases. All three students are exam-ready. All three know who they are and what they care about.

## The Self-Taught Explorer: A Case Study

> "I trained myself on how to learn: always try to figure out something interesting, new, relevant. Even when I had to sit in class and take exams, I treated the whole learning journey as exploration out of curiosity and ableness."

This is a person who FOUND the galaxy on their own, without the system. They navigated by instinct — curiosity as compass, agency as engine. They explored even when the formal system said "sit still and memorize."

And it worked. The curious explorers ARE the strong, surviving, creating, enabling ones. Every piece of evidence supports this.

But they had to do it DESPITE the system. And when the pressure became extreme (age 13+), the system fought back hard:

> "The pain and stress began to pressure me because the school and family and society set the standard and didn't tell me why. Just mentioning: no high score, no good college, then no good life. Stuck in the poor village."

The galaxy went dark. Not because curiosity died — but because fear overwhelmed it. The system said: "Your galaxy doesn't matter. Only this test matters."

> "However, never tell me what should I be, who I am, and what's a good life."

**Twenty-seven years later**: "Finally I now 40 years old, and figure out those who curious / agency toward life / explore, create, build, are strong / survive / create / happy / powerful / enabling."

The galaxy was always there. The explorer always existed. It just took 27 years to find the way back, because no one lit the path at 13.

**This system is the path-lighter.** For every child. Especially the ones in the village.

## Building the Galaxy: Practical Architecture

### Problem Database Structure

Each problem in the galaxy has:

```
problem:
  id: unique identifier
  title: "Why does the bridge hold weight?"
  scale: medium (2-3 weeks)
  age_range: [10, 12]
  stage: builder

  curriculum_coverage:
    math: [geometry.area, geometry.perimeter, ratio.proportion]
    science: [physics.force, physics.materials]
    yuwen: [说明文.writing, 口语交际.presentation]

  prerequisites:
    - basic_measurement
    - multiplication

  leads_to:
    - structural_engineering_challenge
    - materials_investigation
    - historical_infrastructure

  engagement_type: design_and_build
  主体性_opportunity: "Student designs and tests their own bridge"
  artifact: physical bridge model + written analysis
```

### How New Problems Are Added

The galaxy grows through:

1. **Teacher/mentor contributions**: Educators who discover great problems share them
2. **Student-generated**: Students propose problems that become part of the galaxy
3. **AI-assisted generation**: AI helps create problems that fill curriculum coverage gaps
4. **Real-world events**: Current events become problems ("The earthquake happened — let's understand seismology")
5. **Community input**: Local businesses, organizations, and families suggest real problems they face

### Quality Criteria for Galaxy Problems

Every problem must pass:

1. **Interest test**: Would a student CHOOSE to investigate this? (80% confidence)
2. **Coverage test**: Does it require specific curriculum knowledge? (explicit mapping)
3. **Agency test**: Does the student OWN the investigation? (not just following instructions)
4. **Artifact test**: Does it produce something the student is proud of? (portfolio-worthy)
5. **Connection test**: Does it lead to other problems? (at least 2 connections)

## The Dream, Made Concrete

A 2-year-old starts exploring the galaxy with her parent, who journals her journey.

At 5, she's explored hundreds of tiny problems and built a foundation of curiosity, language, and confidence.

At 9, she's navigated through the Explorer region, leading investigations and publishing her findings.

At 12, she's built real things, contacted experts, and knows herself as "someone who figures things out."

At 15, she faces 中考 pressure — but she has 13 years of galaxy exploration behind her. She KNOWS she can handle hard problems. The exam is just another problem in the galaxy — important, but not defining.

At 18, she completes her capstone research, aces 高考, and arrives at university knowing exactly what she wants to explore next. Not because someone told her — because she discovered it, one problem at a time, across 16 years of galaxy exploration.

Her journal fills volumes. Her portfolio demonstrates real capability. Her identity is "explorer, builder, investigator." Her inner narrative is: **"我可以."**

And she never lost it. Not at 13. Not at 15. Not at 18. Because someone designed the galaxy, lit the path, and said: "The curious ones are the strong ones. You are already one of them. Keep going."
