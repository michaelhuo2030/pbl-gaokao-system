# Stage 3: Builder (Ages 10-12, Grades 4-6)

## Developmental Reality

Pre-adolescence. Abstract thinking accelerates. Children can sustain multi-week projects, handle increasing complexity, and begin self-directed learning. Social identity becomes important — "what kind of person am I?" starts to matter.

## Core Mode: Multi-Week Projects With Cross-Subject Integration

Projects span 3-6 weeks. Students manage timelines, divide labor, and produce substantial artifacts. Subjects are integrated: a single project can cover math, science, 语文, and social studies simultaneously.

## What 体制内 Requires (Grades 4-6)

### 语文
- ~2500 characters, reading comprehension of complex texts
- Essay writing (记叙文, 说明文)
- Classical Chinese introduction (文言文 basics)
- Advanced poetry appreciation

### Math
- Fractions, decimals, percentages
- Area, volume, basic geometry proofs
- Ratio and proportion
- Basic statistics, probability introduction
- Algebraic thinking (pre-algebra)

### English
- Reading passages, grammar, basic writing
- Vocabulary expansion, tenses

### Science
- Life science, earth science, physical science
- Experimental design basics

### Social Studies (道德与法治)
- Chinese history basics, geography, civic awareness

## The Transformation: Multi-Subject Problem Sets

### "City Redesign" Project (6 weeks)

**Mission**: "Our neighborhood has a problem: [traffic / flooding / lack of green space / aging population]. Research it, propose a solution, present to a community panel."

**Subjects covered**:
- **Math**: Calculate areas, percentages of land use, budget estimates, scale drawings, data collection and statistics
- **语文**: Write a research report (说明文), interview residents (listening + speaking), persuasive presentation (口语交际)
- **Science**: Environmental science, water systems, urban ecology
- **Social Studies**: Community governance, civic participation, Chinese urban planning history
- **English**: Read international case studies of similar problems
- **Art**: Design presentation materials, scale models

**高考 knowledge points hit**: Area/volume calculation, percentage, data representation, 说明文 structure, persuasive writing, environmental science concepts — all in one project.

### "Historical Mystery" Project (4 weeks)

**Mission**: "Something happened in Chinese history that changed everything. You're the detective. Research it, reconstruct the events, present the case."

**Subjects covered**:
- **语文**: Read primary sources (文言文 introduction!), write narrative account (记叙文)
- **Math**: Timeline calculations, population data analysis
- **Social Studies**: Deep historical understanding through investigation
- **English**: Compare with events in other countries

Students encounter 文言文 because they NEED to read an old document, not because it's Chapter 7.

## The 80/20 Principle in Practice

Following the insight that motivation is the primary driver:

- **80% of design effort**: Making the problem genuinely interesting, relevant, and owned by students
- **20% of design effort**: Ensuring knowledge coverage and skill scaffolding

If a project doesn't ignite genuine interest, it fails — regardless of how well-aligned it is with curriculum standards. The scaffolding is there, but the engine is motivation.

## Journaling as Core Practice

At this stage, journaling becomes a powerful metacognitive tool:

### Builder's Journal (建造者日志)

Every student maintains a journal that captures:

1. **Observation**: "What did I notice today?" (developing the Presencing skill of seeing)
2. **Connection**: "How does this connect to what I already know?"
3. **Process**: "What did I try? What worked? What didn't?"
4. **Next Move**: "What will I investigate tomorrow?"
5. **Agency Marker**: "Today I figured out... / I can now..."

This is NOT graded. It is reviewed (by student and mentor) for patterns:
- What ignites this student's curiosity?
- Where do they get stuck? Why?
- How is their sense of agency developing?
- What problems do they naturally gravitate toward?

The journal is the instrument for understanding each student's motivation landscape — the same way a parent might journal observations of a young child, but now the student does it themselves.

## 主体性 at Ages 10-12

Agency deepens from "I can figure this out" to "I can lead this":

- **"我负责..."** (I'm responsible for...) — taking ownership of project components
- **"我的方法是..."** (My approach is...) — developing personal methods
- **"这个有问题，因为..."** (This is wrong because...) — critical evaluation, including of their own work

### Concrete Practices

1. **Project proposals**: Students pitch project ideas. Not all are accepted — they learn to refine and defend.
2. **Peer review with standards**: Students evaluate each other's work against rubrics they helped create.
3. **Self-assessment before teacher assessment**: "How do you think you did? Why?" — always first.
4. **Expert connections**: Students contact real experts (email, visit) for their projects. Adults don't gatekeep knowledge.
5. **Failure documentation**: Journal entry for every significant mistake: what happened, why, what you learned.

## AI Integration

**AI becomes a visible research and thinking tool:**

- Students use AI for research — but must verify with at least one other source
- AI helps with brainstorming ("What are possible causes of urban flooding?")
- Students practice **prompting as a skill**: good questions → better AI responses
- **Critical rule**: AI-generated text is never submitted as student work. AI helps think; students do the work.
- Introduction of "AI audit": students evaluate AI answers for accuracy and bias

## Assessment

### Project-Based Evidence

| Curriculum Standard | Evidence Source |
|---|---|
| 说明文 writing | Published research report from City Redesign project |
| Fraction/percentage operations | Budget calculations with documented work |
| Scientific method | Experiment logs from investigation projects |
| 文言文 comprehension | Annotated primary source from Historical Mystery project |

### Diagnostic Checks (Monthly)

Brief, curriculum-aligned diagnostics identify gaps. Not punitive — feed into next project cycle. Students see their own data and set personal goals.

### Portfolio Reviews (Semester)

Students select their best work, write reflections, and present to parents/mentors. The question is always: "What can you do now that you couldn't before?"

## What Success Looks Like at Age 12

- [ ] Can manage a multi-week project with minimal adult intervention
- [ ] Writes substantive essays in multiple formats
- [ ] Uses math as a tool for real analysis, not just exercise completion
- [ ] Has conducted genuine investigations with real-world data
- [ ] Can use AI as a research tool while maintaining critical judgment
- [ ] Maintains a journal that shows growing self-awareness
- [ ] Has an identity as "someone who figures things out" — not "a good student who follows instructions"
- [ ] Ready for the increased rigor of middle school without anxiety
