# Resource Requirements and AI Facilitation Analysis

## The Question

Traditional PBL requires resources that most schools don't have: well-trained facilitators, mentors, consistent curricula, expert connections. This has always been the equity risk (see `risk/risk-analysis.md`, Risk 7).

But in 2026, the question has changed: **how much of this can AI systems — well-engineered ones — actually provide or dramatically reduce the cost of?**

The answer, on examination, is: nearly everything. Not perfectly. Not without human involvement. But enough to make the system accessible to schools and families that could never afford the traditional PBL resource requirements.

## Must-Have Resources

These are non-negotiable. Without them, the system cannot function.

| Resource | What It Means | AI Facilitation Potential | Difficulty | What's Required |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **PBL-trained facilitators** | Teachers who can design problems, facilitate inquiry, and guide without lecturing. The #1 bottleneck. | **Very High**. AI can: (1) provide real-time facilitation coaching during class, (2) generate problem designs from curriculum standards, (3) model Socratic questioning for teachers to practice with, (4) analyze classroom recordings and give feedback. The teacher still needs to be present and human — but the TRAINING can be largely AI-powered. | Medium | AI coaching system + curated training scenarios + feedback loops. Teachers need basic digital literacy and willingness to be coached. |
| **Tutor/teacher self-training system** | Ongoing professional development — teachers continuously improving their PBL facilitation skills. | **Very High**. AI can simulate students with different needs, generate practice scenarios, provide personalized feedback on facilitation style, and track growth over time. A teacher can "practice-teach" with AI students at midnight if they want. This is the single biggest unlock: training that scales without trainers. | Medium | AI role-play system (AI acts as student), rubrics for good facilitation, mentor teacher review of AI-flagged moments. |
| **Curriculum-aligned problem banks** | Libraries of well-designed problems mapped to every 高考/中考 knowledge point. | **Very High**. AI can generate problems from curriculum standards, cross-reference with knowledge point maps, adjust difficulty, and localize to different contexts (rural vs urban). Human review needed for quality, but the generation is 10x faster. | Low-Medium | Knowledge point database + problem templates + human quality review process. |
| **Diagnostic assessment system** | Monthly checks that identify knowledge gaps per student. | **Very High**. AI can generate assessments, auto-grade, analyze patterns, identify individual and class-wide gaps, and recommend which gaps to address through which projects. Nearly fully automatable. | Low | Question bank + adaptive testing engine + reporting dashboard. Existing EdTech can do most of this. |
| **Curriculum coverage mapping** | Proof that every 高考 standard is addressed. Parents need this for trust. | **Very High**. AI can auto-map problems to standards, generate coverage reports, identify gaps, and suggest which problems to add. The mapping itself can be largely automated. | Low | National curriculum standards in structured format + mapping algorithm + human verification. |
| **Journaling/mentoring system** | Structured reflection practice with adult review. Core to 主体性 development. | **High**. The human mentor is still essential for the relationship — but AI dramatically reduces the analytical burden. | Medium | NLP journal analysis system + privacy-respecting design + mentor training on using AI insights. |

But the environment itself requires human presence and design. | High | Physical space, trained adults, cultural norms. AI is supplementary here, not primary. |

AI can generate personalized parent reports showing: what their child explored, which knowledge points were covered, how they compare to standards, what's coming next. Real-time dashboards possible. Reduces the "what are you doing with my child?" anxiety. | Low-Medium | Reporting templates + data pipeline from assessments + multilingual support. |

## Better-to-Have Resources

These significantly improve the system but aren't absolute requirements. Schools without them can still run the core system.

| Resource | What It Means | AI Facilitation Potential | Difficulty | What's Required |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Expert/mentor connections** | Students access real professionals for projects (e.g., interview an engineer, visit a lab). | **High**. AI can simulate expert conversations for practice, provide domain expertise through chat, and connect students to virtual expert networks. Not the same as meeting a real person — but eliminates the geographic barrier entirely. A rural student can "talk to" a marine biologist at midnight. | Low-Medium | Domain-expert AI personas (well-prompted, grounded in real knowledge) + occasional real expert video calls. |
| **Cross-age mentoring program** | Older students mentor younger ones. Deepens understanding for both. | **Medium**. AI can facilitate matching, provide mentoring guides, and help older students prepare explanations. But the human relationship IS the point. AI is logistics, not substitute. | Medium | Scheduling system + mentoring frameworks + teacher oversight. |
| **Community presentation panels** | External audiences for student work (parents, local officials, professionals). | **Medium**. AI can help students prepare presentations, simulate tough questions, and provide feedback. But real audiences matter for motivation. AI can help organize and invite panelists. | Medium | Community relationships + event logistics. AI helps with prep, not replacement. |
| **Virtual simulations / lab access** | Experiences that geography limits: virtual chemistry labs, physics simulations, virtual field trips. | **Very High**. This is already well-developed EdTech. AI can create custom simulations tied to specific project problems. A school without a chemistry lab can still run experiments virtually. Major equity unlock. | Low-Medium | Existing simulation platforms + AI-generated custom scenarios + basic device access (phone/tablet sufficient). |
| **Field experience / transportation** | "Cheat transportation" — students visit different environments regardless of location. | **Medium**. AI can provide virtual field trips with rich, interactive content. VR/AR is emerging. Not the same as being there, but far better than nothing. Physical transportation still matters for some experiences. | Medium-High | VR/AR content + devices, or physical transportation subsidy. |
| **AI tools for student use** | Students using AI as research/thinking tool (Stages 3-5). | **Very High** (self-referential — this IS AI facilitation). The tool IS the resource. Key requirement: age-appropriate guardrails, transparency norms, and teacher training on guiding AI use. | Low | API access or consumer AI tools + usage guidelines + transparency logging. |
| **Parent education program** | Workshops helping parents understand PBL and learning science. | **Very High**. AI can deliver personalized parent education: answer questions 24/7, explain the science behind PBL, show their child's specific data, address anxieties in real time. A "parent AI coach" that knows the system deeply. | Low-Medium | Well-prompted parent-facing AI + FAQ database + escalation to human staff for complex concerns. |
| **Community of practice for teachers** | Teachers learning from each other across schools. No school implements alone. | **High**. AI can facilitate knowledge sharing: match teachers facing similar challenges, summarize what worked at other schools, create shared resource libraries, and moderate discussion forums. Reduces isolation of pioneer teachers. | Low-Medium | Platform for teacher community + AI-curated best practices + regional meetup coordination. |
| **Dedicated exam training materials** | 高考/中考-specific practice with strategic analysis. | **Very High**. AI can generate unlimited practice questions, provide instant detailed feedback, analyze error patterns, and create personalized study plans. This is where AI is already strongest. | Low | Exam question database + adaptive practice engine + strategy analysis. Largely exists already. |

## The AI Facilitation Summary

### What AI Can Nearly Fully Provide (Low difficulty, Very High potential)
- Curriculum mapping and coverage verification
- Diagnostic assessments and gap analysis
- Problem bank generation
- Exam training materials
- Parent communication and reporting

### What AI Can Dramatically Improve (Medium difficulty, High potential)
- Teacher/tutor self-training (the biggest unlock)
- Journaling analysis and mentor support
- Expert access simulation
- Virtual lab/field experiences
- Parent education
- Teacher community of practice

### What AI Cannot Replace (High difficulty, human-essential)
- Safe psychological environments
- Real human relationships (mentor-student, parent-child, peer-peer)
- Physical exploration and sensory experience
- Community trust and cultural norms
- The co-pilot relationship itself

### The Key Insight

The traditional PBL resource gap was:

> "We need trained teachers, mentors, experts, labs, transportation, and curricular support. Most schools can't afford this. PBL remains a privilege."

The AI-era PBL resource gap becomes:

> "We need basic devices, internet access, well-engineered AI systems, and at least one willing adult facilitator per classroom. The AI handles most of the training, content, assessment, and expert access."

This is not a small difference. It is the difference between a system that works in 50 elite schools and a system that can scale to 500,000 schools.

## The Stage-by-Stage Summary

| Stage | AI Coverage | Human Irreducible Core | Net Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Seedling (2-5) | ~80% | Physical nature, real presence, genuine co-piloting | A parent with a phone can run most of this stage |
| Explorer (6-8) | ~70% | Teacher's human presence, classroom culture, mistake-safe environment | A willing teacher + AI = effective PBL classroom |
| Builder (9-12) | ~75% | Mentor relationship, identity work, portfolio celebrations | AI handles analysis; human handles connection |
| Investigator (13-15) | ~60% | Mentor conviction, psychological safety, crisis intervention, genuine belief | AI is support infrastructure for the most critical human work |
| Architect (16-18) | ~70% | Post-exam identity, health protection, real conviction that scores ≠ worth | AI makes exam prep efficient so humans can focus on the whole person |

**The pattern**: AI coverage is lowest at the crisis point (age 13-15) — exactly when the human element matters most. This is not a weakness of AI. It is a DESIGN PRINCIPLE: AI handles the scalable work so humans can focus their limited energy on the irreplaceable work — the moments that save students.

## The Tutor Self-Training Deep Dive

This deserves special attention because it solves the #1 bottleneck: teacher capability.

### How AI-Powered Tutor Self-Training Works

1. **AI Student Simulator**: The AI plays a student — with realistic misconceptions, emotions, and responses. The teacher practices facilitating.
   - "I don't understand fractions" (basic)
   - "Why do I need to learn this?" (motivation challenge)
   - "The AI already gave me the answer" (AI dependency scenario)
   - Student who is bored, student who is anxious, student who is disruptive

2. **Real-Time Coaching**: During actual classes (with consent), AI listens and provides private feedback to the teacher:
   - "You answered the student's question directly. Try asking a counter-question next time."
   - "Three students haven't spoken in 20 minutes. Consider a turn-and-talk."
   - "The problem design is covering algebra but missing the geometry connection."

3. **Reflection and Growth Tracking**: After each session:
   - AI summarizes what the teacher did well and what to try differently
   - Tracks improvement over weeks/months
   - Suggests specific skills to practice next
   - Connects teacher to resources (videos of master facilitators, articles, peer teachers who excel in that area)

4. **Peer Learning Network**: AI matches teachers who are strong in one area with teachers who need help in that area. Creates structured peer-coaching pairs.

### What's Required
- A well-engineered AI system (not just a chatbot — a system designed for teacher development)
- Audio/video classroom recording infrastructure (phone is sufficient)
- Privacy framework (student consent, data protection)
- Mentor teacher oversight (AI coaching is reviewed by experienced human mentors)
- Cultural sensitivity (Chinese classroom norms, face-saving, respect hierarchies)

### Difficulty: Medium
The technology exists. The engineering is substantial but not breakthrough. The main challenge is cultural adoption: teachers must be willing to be coached by AI, which requires trust and face-safe design.

## Conclusion

The resource table reveals something profound: **the system that seemed impossibly expensive to scale is, in 2026, becoming affordable to scale.** Not because the requirements changed, but because AI can fulfill most of them at marginal cost.

The remaining human requirements — a willing facilitator, a safe environment, real relationships — are the irreducible core. Everything else is engineering.

And the most powerful unlock of all: **teachers training themselves, with AI, at their own pace, in their own context.** This is 主体性 for teachers. The same principle applied to the adults in the system. Everyone is an explorer in the galaxy.
