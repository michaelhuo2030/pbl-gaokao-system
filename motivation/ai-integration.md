# AI Integration Philosophy

## The Core Position

AI is the most powerful learning tool since the printing press. It is also the most powerful motivation-killer if misused.

This system's approach: **AI amplifies agency, never replaces it.**

A student who uses AI well is more capable than one who doesn't. A student who depends on AI has less 主体性 than one who never encountered it. The integration must navigate this tension deliberately at every age.

## The Guiding Question

At every stage, ask: **Does this use of AI increase or decrease the student's sense of "我可以"?**

- AI helps me research faster → "我可以 investigate more deeply" ✓
- AI writes my essay for me → "我可以... wait, what did I actually do?" ✗
- AI explains a concept I'm struggling with → "我可以 understand this now" ✓
- AI solves my math problem → "我可以... type numbers into a box?" ✗
- AI generates practice problems at my level → "我可以 challenge myself" ✓
- AI completes my project → "我可以... take credit for nothing?" ✗

## Integration by Stage

### Stage 1: Seedling (Ages 2-5) — AI is Invisible

**For the child**: No AI interaction. The real world is the curriculum.

**For parents/educators**: AI serves as a behind-the-scenes assistant:
- Generate age-appropriate exploration prompts ("Ask your child: what do you think will happen if...")
- Understand developmental milestones ("Is it normal that my 3-year-old...")
- Design responsive environments ("Given she's interested in bugs, try...")
- Analyze observation journals for patterns ("Based on your logs, she seems most engaged when...")

**Principle**: Children this age need human connection, sensory experience, and real objects. Screens are not learning tools for toddlers.

---

### Stage 2: Explorer (Ages 6-9) — AI as Occasional Reference

**For the child**: Very limited, supervised AI use:
- "We found a strange bug. Let's ask the AI what kind it is." (identification tool)
- "What does this word mean?" (dictionary function)
- AI is positioned as "one of many sources" — alongside books, observation, and people

**For parents/educators**: AI assists with:
- Generating differentiated problem sets ("She's ahead on fractions but struggling with word problems")
- Creating personalized reading recommendations
- Designing projects aligned with individual interests
- Journal analysis support

**Rules**:
- AI is always used WITH an adult present at this age
- Students learn: "AI can be wrong. How would you check?"
- No AI for creative work (writing, art) — that's the student's domain

---

### Stage 3: Builder (Ages 10-12) — AI as Research Assistant

**For the student**: AI becomes a regular research tool with clear boundaries:

**Allowed**:
- Research questions: "What are the causes of urban flooding?"
- Concept explanation: "Explain how photosynthesis works in simpler terms"
- Brainstorming: "What are 10 possible solutions to [problem]?"
- Translation/language help for English sources
- Checking understanding: "Am I right that [concept] means [explanation]?"

**Not allowed**:
- Writing any part of the student's work product
- Solving math problems (AI can explain concepts, not do homework)
- Making decisions for the student ("Which topic should I choose?")

**New Skill**: Prompting
- Students learn that good questions → better AI responses
- Practiced explicitly: "How could you ask this question to get a more useful answer?"

**New Skill**: Verification
- "AI said X. Find another source that confirms or contradicts it."
- Weekly "AI fact-check" exercise: find something AI got wrong

---

### Stage 4: Investigator (Ages 13-15) — AI as Thinking Partner

**For the student**: AI becomes a sophisticated intellectual tool:

**Allowed (everything from Stage 3, plus)**:
- Draft feedback: "Here's my essay draft. What's weak about my argument?"
- Data analysis assistance for project work
- Research synthesis: "Summarize the key findings from these 5 sources"
- Debate practice: "Argue the opposite position so I can prepare my defense"
- Study support: "Quiz me on [topic] and tell me what I'm missing"

**New Skill**: Critical AI Evaluation
- Students analyze AI-generated content for accuracy, bias, and gaps
- "AI Audit" sessions: compare AI output to expert sources
- Students articulate what AI is good at and what it's bad at in each subject

**Transparency Rule**:
All AI use is documented in project reports:
> "AI assisted with initial research synthesis (I verified all claims with primary sources)"
> "AI generated practice problems for my exam prep (I solved them independently)"

**New Skill**: Knowing When NOT to Use AI
- "For this part of the project, using AI would bypass the thinking I need to do"
- Students develop judgment about when AI helps and when it hinders learning

---

### Stage 5: Architect (Ages 16-18) — AI as Full Collaborator

**For the student**: Sophisticated, mature AI use that mirrors professional practice:

**Full collaboration**:
- AI-augmented research for capstone projects
- AI as analysis partner for complex data
- AI for exam preparation: adaptive practice, concept clarification, weak-point identification
- AI for time management and study planning
- AI for exploring university/career options with data

**Meta-level skills**:
- Students can articulate AI's capabilities and limitations in their field of study
- Students make strategic decisions about when to use AI and when to think independently
- Students can identify when AI output is superficially plausible but wrong
- Students understand how AI models work (at a conceptual level)

**Capstone Integration**:
- Some students may investigate AI itself as a capstone topic
- Students contribute to AI discourse as informed participants, not just consumers

**Exam Season Specific**:
- AI generates targeted practice problems based on diagnostic results
- AI explains concepts in multiple ways when student is stuck
- AI does NOT replace the student's own problem-solving — it supplements
- Students own their study strategy; AI provides data and tools

---

## AI Ethics Across All Stages

### What We Teach About AI

1. **AI is a tool, not a person** — It doesn't "think" or "want"
2. **AI reflects its training data** — Including biases and errors
3. **AI output requires judgment** — The human evaluates, not the other way around
4. **AI is not a shortcut to understanding** — Using AI to avoid thinking defeats the purpose
5. **Honesty about AI use** — Transparent documentation is a non-negotiable norm

### The Academic Integrity Framework

| Stage | Standard |
|---|---|
| Explorer (6-9) | AI is a reference tool (like a dictionary). Work is the student's. |
| Builder (10-12) | AI assists research. All writing and problem-solving is the student's. AI use is noted. |
| Investigator (13-15) | AI assists thinking. Student produces all final work. AI use is documented with specifics. |
| Architect (16-18) | AI collaborates on process. Student is responsible for all intellectual claims and final products. Full transparency. |

### The Test: Could You Do It Without AI?

At every stage, students should be able to answer: "If AI disappeared tomorrow, could I still do this?"

- If YES → AI is being used as an amplifier ✓
- If NO → AI is being used as a crutch ✗

This test is applied explicitly in journal reflections and mentor conversations.

## For the AI Era: What Remains Human

This system prepares students for a world where AI handles routine cognitive tasks. What remains uniquely human:

1. **Framing problems** — AI can solve problems; humans decide which problems matter
2. **Making meaning** — AI can analyze; humans decide what it means
3. **Ethical judgment** — AI can optimize; humans decide what to optimize for
4. **Creative vision** — AI can generate; humans decide what's worth creating
5. **Relational depth** — AI can communicate; humans connect
6. **主体性** — AI can act; humans choose to act and own the consequences

Every element of this PBL system builds these capabilities. AI integration serves them, never undermines them.
